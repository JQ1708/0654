(c) The balanced equation for the reaction in (b) is shown below. 2HCl(aq) + CaCO₃(s) → CaCl₂(aq) + CO₂(g) + H₂O(l)

(i) State the meanings of the state symbols (aq) and (l).

Tap to Reveal Answer

(aq): aqueous / dissolved in water.

(l): liquid.

(ii) Use steps 1, 2 and 3 to calculate the volume of carbon dioxide that is produced when 2.0g of calcium carbonate reacts with excess dilute hydrochloric acid. Show your working.

Tap to Reveal Answer

Step 1: Calculate the number of moles of calcium carbonate contained in 2.0g. [Aᵣ: Ca, 40; C, 12; O, 16]

Mᵣ of CaCO₃ = 40 + 12 + (3 x 16) = 100. Moles = 2.0⁄100 = 0.02 moles.

Volume = 0.02 x 24 = 0.48 dm³.

calcium carbonate + hydrochloric acid → calcium chloride + water + carbon dioxide

He needs a gas syringe connected to the flask to measure the volume of CO₂ collected over time. Alternatively, he could use an electronic balance to measure the loss in mass of the flask and contents over time.

(ii) He carries out the experiment using dilute hydrochloric acid at 20 °C. He repeats the experiment at 30 °C. Fig. 11.2 shows his results.

Space for Fig. 11.2 graphs for 20°C and 30°C.

State and explain the differences between the graphs in terms of the movement of reacting particles.

Tap to Reveal Answer

At the higher temperature (30 °C), the initial rate of reaction is higher (steeper graph). This is because the particles have more kinetic energy, leading to a greater frequency of collisions. Additionally, more particles possess the minimum activation energy required for a successful collision.

(b) Lead chloride is an insoluble salt. Describe a method of making solid lead chloride from reactants chosen from Table 11.1.

compoundsolubility in water
calcium chloridesoluble
lead nitratesoluble
sodium chloridesoluble
Tap to Reveal Answer

Reactants: Lead nitrate and either calcium chloride or sodium chloride.

(c) Lead chloride has a melting point of 501 °C. Explain how the structure of lead chloride causes it to have a high melting point.

Tap to Reveal Answer

Lead chloride has a giant ionic lattice structure. There are strong electrostatic forces of attraction between the positive lead ions and negative chloride ions. A large amount of energy is required to overcome these strong forces, resulting in a high melting point.
