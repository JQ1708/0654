Tap to Reveal Answer

N₂ + O₂ → 2NO

(ii) Explain why the rate of this reaction increases as the temperature inside the car engine increases. Use ideas about collisions between particles.

Tap to Reveal Answer

At a higher temperature, particles have more kinetic energy and move faster. This increases the rate of successful collisions because more particles have the required activation energy to react.

An increased concentration means there are more particles per unit volume. This leads to an increased rate of successful collisions between the reacting particles.

(b) Aqueous copper(II) sulfate conducts electricity. Explain why.

It conducts electricity because it contains ions (Cu²⁺, SO₄²⁻, H⁺, OH⁻) which are free to move and carry charge.

(c) Fig. 5.1 shows the apparatus used for the electrolysis of aqueous copper(II) sulfate.

Space for Fig. 5.1 electrolysis apparatus.

(i) State the name given to the positive electrode.

Tap to Reveal Answer

Anode

(ii) The purification (refining) of copper uses electrolysis. Describe how impure copper is purified by electrolysis. Include ionic half-equations in your answer.

Tap to Reveal Answer

The electrolyte used is a solution of copper(II) sulfate. The impure copper is made the anode (positive electrode) and a thin sheet of pure copper is made the cathode (negative electrode). At the anode, copper atoms lose electrons and dissolve into the solution as ions: Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻. At the cathode, copper ions from the solution gain electrons and are deposited as pure copper: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu.

(b) Potassium sulfate, K₂SO₄, is a fertiliser that contains potassium. A student makes some potassium sulfate by reacting potassium carbonate, K₂CO₃, with sulfuric acid. K₂CO₃ + H₂SO₄ → K₂SO₄ + CO₂ + H₂O. The student uses 2.76g of potassium carbonate. Calculate the mass of potassium sulfate the student makes. [Aᵣ: C, 12; H, 1; K, 39; O, 16; S, 32]

Tap to Reveal Answer

(b) For each factor (temperature and concentration): describe how the rate of reaction changes and explain why, using ideas about particles.

Space for Fig. 5.1 showing particle arrangements for solid, liquid, gas.
Tap to Reveal Answer

Gas: Particles are far apart and randomly arranged.

Solid: Particles are closely packed in a regular, ordered arrangement.

(b) (i) Liquid water boils at 100 °C to form steam. Describe what happens to the water particles during this change of state.

Tap to Reveal Answer

Arrangement: The particles move further apart and become randomly arranged.

Movement: The particles move more freely and randomly at high speeds.

(ii) Explain why the bonds between atoms are not broken when water boils.

Tap to Reveal Answer

The covalent bonds between the hydrogen and oxygen atoms within the water molecules are very strong and require a large amount of energy to break, whereas the intermolecular forces between the molecules are much weaker.

(iii) Draw a dot-and-cross diagram to show the bonding in water, H₂O. Show only the outer shell electrons.

Tap to Reveal Answer

The diagram should show the oxygen atom sharing one pair of electrons with each of the two hydrogen atoms. The oxygen atom should also have two lone pairs (four non-bonding electrons) in its outer shell.

(c) (i) The reaction between water and magnesium is faster if hot water is used. Explain why. Use ideas about collisions between particles.

Tap to Reveal Answer

At a higher temperature, the particles move faster and have more kinetic energy. This means more particles possess the minimum activation energy to react upon collision, and collisions are more frequent.

(ii) The reaction between water and magnesium is faster if powdered magnesium is used instead of strips of magnesium. Explain why. Use ideas about collisions between particles.

Tap to Reveal Answer

Powdered magnesium has a larger surface area than a strip of the same mass. This exposes more magnesium particles to the water, leading to more frequent collisions and a faster reaction rate.
